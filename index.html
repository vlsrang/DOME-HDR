Abstract

One pipeline, two consistent outputs

We propose DOME-HDR, a dual-output multi-exposure HDR reconstruction framework that jointly produces a perceptually balanced SDR image and a consistent HDR image via gain map inverse tone mapping. Given three bracketed LDR inputs, DOME-HDR first synthesizes a base SDR using a LoRA-adapted latent diffusion model. A dual cross-attention fusion module injects complementary structural and color cues from the under- and over-exposed images while anchoring on the mid-exposure for stability. The synthesized SDR then guides HPGM, our HDR Prior-guided Gain Map network, to predict a spatially varying gain map for reliable dynamic-range expansion. Evaluated on Kalantari, Tel, and Challenge123 with both full-reference and no-reference metrics, DOME-HDR achieves state-of-the-art HDR reconstruction quality; ablations further confirm the effectiveness of dual cross-attention and SDR-guided gain map estimation.
Method

Why split the problem in two

Prior gain-map methods assume the input SDR already preserves scene structure — a gain map can only scale existing signal, never recover what saturation destroyed. DOME-HDR instead optimizes a strong SDR generator and a gain-map estimator jointly, so errors from one stage don't silently propagate into the other.

STAGE 1

Base SDR Synthesis

Built on a LoRA-adapted UltraFusion backbone. The mid-exposure image anchors the diffusion latent for stability, while two independent cross-attention branches inject structural and color cues from the under- and over-exposed images — avoiding the information loss of naively averaging guidance features before attention.

STAGE 2

HPGM — Prior-Guided Gain Map

A U-Net conditioned on an 11-channel input: the synthesized SDR, cross-exposure ratios, and highlight/shadow saturation masks. An HDR prior built from all three exposures is injected at the bottleneck; the network predicts a residual over that prior plus a global HDR scale.

Training runs in two phases: Phase 1 jointly trains the LoRA diffusion module and HPGM against ground-truth-anchored SDRs; Phase 2 re-optimizes HPGM on the model's own generated SDRs, closing the train/inference domain gap.

Skipping the tone-mapping lottery

Conventional HDR pipelines need a tone mapping operator for display, and perceptual quality swings with the operator chosen. Because DOME-HDR emits a display-ready SDR directly from Stage 1, it sidesteps that variance entirely.

Ablations

What each design choice earns

Joint training vs. cascade

Feeding a pretrained UltraFusion SDR into a pretrained GMNet, with no joint optimization, collapses quality; naively training both modules jointly from scratch is worse still, due to conflicting SDR/HDR gradients.

PU-PSNR: 13.82 (naive joint) → 22.96 (cascade) → 44.63 (ours)

Dual cross-attention

Separate attention branches for under- and over-exposed guidance outperform UltraFusion's normalized-summation fusion across every metric.

LPIPS 0.0180 → 0.0156, DISTS 0.0157 → 0.0147

HDR prior inputs

Gain estimation from the SDR alone is weak; adding either the under- or over-exposed image helps, but only all three exposures give reliable radiometric coverage.

PU-PSNR: 23.69 (SDR only) → 44.63 (all three)

Two-phase training

Phase 1 alone gives a strong initialization; Phase 2 alone sharpens perceptual metrics on generated SDRs. Combined, the model surpasses AFUNet on nearly every metric.

PU-PSNR: 40.53 (P1) / 43.26 (P2) / 44.63 (P1+P2)
Limitation. Reconstruction quality is bounded by how reliable the exposure cues are: severe motion, large saturated regions, or complex illumination can degrade the fused SDR and, downstream, the gain estimate — so recovering extreme radiance variations in those regions remains difficult. The authors point to more expressive exposure representations as future work.
